Office Entry 1

By John Holland

Published on December 9, 2025

Last Edited: December 9, 2025

I work at a bank. I won’t identify this bank as they’ve gotten to cracking down on us malcontents writing online. It’s a large bank. I pull into the parking lot on a rainy Monday morning, and no matter what parking spot I pull in there is invariably someone idling in their car just waiting to muster up the energy to get out. It’s a large complex with an unending number of potential parking spaces, so statistically the odds of this happening every time I pull in seems low. There must be something appealing about waiting in your car before going in, but I usually just get out right away. The building I work in employs nearly 7,000 people and they work in many different areas, however they segment the building so that like work with like. The parking lot and the entrance however, are the shared spaces before the filter. You see mainly two types, the call center part-timers and the corporate full-timers. You always know the difference between the two. I’m of the latter category, which means I get off on floor 3 (while they got off on floor 2). If you are concerned that I hold judgmental attitudes towards the part-timers, you’d be mistaken. I actually have a respect for them. They operate as I think you should. Work is instrumental for them, they don’t dress any differently then they do outside of work. Flat-billed hat, hoodies, Jordans, graphic tees, ripped jeans. There’s obviously more variation in outfit than that, but that is the general trend. The full-timers on the other hand don’t vary in dress at all. Button-up shirt, khakis. Add a quarter zip or winter coat when the weather gets colder. Beyond appearance and their parking lot behaviors, I can tell you no more about the part-timers. I make my way through the parking lot, making passing glances at the 2006 Nissans, 2024 Toyotas, a couple Mercedes, and of course Indians in Teslas. I scan into the building and make my way into the elevator pressing the floor 3 button. Here we enter the area that I spent the majority of my waking hours. A newly renovated and corporate-slick office space, shared sections that no one uses, funny looking chairs across from coffee makers, meeting rooms, and open concept cubicles.
